The Last New Genre

A field note on SoundCloud rap, COVID, and an algorithm that never asked for something new.

Every few years, popular music used to produce something with no name yet. Then, sometime around 2019, it stopped. Here's the actual mechanism behind why, not just the feeling that things got worse.

Jul 21, 2026 · Justin Ray · ~12 min read

For most of pop music's history, you could set a clock by it. Every few years, something arrived that hadn't existed before: a new sound, attached to a new scene, that eventually got a name and a mythology. Rock and roll in the '50s. Soul, psychedelia, and funk in the '60s. Disco, punk, reggae's crossover, and hip-hop out of the Bronx in the '70s. House out of Chicago and techno out of Detroit in the '80s. Grunge, jungle, gangsta rap, and trip-hop in the '90s. Dubstep and grime out of South and East London, crunk out of the South, in the 2000s. Cloud rap, witch house, vaporwave, and trap's mainstream takeover in the early 2010s.

Then, starting around 2015, it happened one more time. SoundCloud rap: teenagers with face tattoos and no A&R department, uploading straight to the platform, building an aesthetic (emo-inflected, blown-out on purpose, equal parts sadness and chaos) that hadn't existed before. It had its own sound, its own scene, its own stars: Lil Peep, XXXTentacion, Juice WRLD, Lil Pump, 6ix9ine. Big enough that music outlets were writing its obituary within about two years of writing its birth announcement, and several of its most important young artists didn't live to see it happen.

By 2019, that obituary was basically finalized. Culture writers were already calling the SoundCloud rap era formally over, its raw DIY energy either co-opted by major labels or fossilized into a look you'd buy at Hot Topic. A question that had been circulating quietly in music forums for a while, why hasn't there been a new genre in years?, got a lot louder. One forum thread from January 2020, right on the seam between the two eras, laid it out almost too cleanly: rock since the '50s, hip-hop and reggae since the '70s, grunge in the '90s, then the 2000s brought nothing, the 2010s brought nothing, and nothing visible on the horizon for the 2020s either.

That's the claim worth taking seriously. SoundCloud rap as the last genre a mass audience could agree was genuinely new. Not a subgenre, not a rebrand, not a sound with a hashtag, but a new thing with a name that a stranger on the internet would recognize on sight. Then COVID hit two months after that forum post, and by most tellings, whatever was left of the process just stopped.

I think this is basically right, for more specific and more interesting reasons than "everyone got lazy" or "there's nothing left to invent." Something did break in 2019. COVID made it worse. But what actually broke wasn't anyone's ability to make a new sound. It was the machinery that used to turn a new sound into a genre. Once you see what that machinery actually was, the same collapse starts showing up in how people date and how they argue online too.

Genres Are Not Sounds. They're Institutions.

The mistake in most "genres are dead" arguments is treating genre as a purely sonic category, like it's just about which frequencies and drum patterns are new. It isn't. A genre is a sound plus a scene that tests it, argues over it, and repeats it enough times that it hardens into a shared vocabulary. House music wasn't just 909 drum machines. It was Frankie Knuckles at the Warehouse, playing the same tracks for the same crowd for months until a whole room agreed on what "it" was. Jersey club wasn't just a tempo and a bed-squeak sample. It was Newark DJs building it live, night after night, in front of dancers who told them immediately, with their bodies, whether it worked. Grime wasn't just 140 BPM and sub-bass. It was pirate radio in East London, where MCs tested bars against each other in real time and the audience was right there in the room, or on the other end of the signal.

New genres have almost always been born out of physical, repeated, collective friction: a scene testing a sound against a live crowd, over and over, until enough people agree it deserves a name. That process is slow, it's local, and for a while it sounds bad, before enough repetition makes it sound inevitable. Punk sounded like a mistake in 1976. So did jungle in 1992. So did SoundCloud rap's blown-out vocals in 2016. The badness is part of the mechanism. It's the sound of a scene still arguing with itself before the argument resolves into a genre.

2019: The Boundaries Already Half-Dissolved

The first stage happened before COVID, and before SoundCloud rap even finished dying. Through the 2010s, streaming and infinite internet access did something specific to listeners: it stopped organizing them into tribes. You didn't have to be a metalhead or a raver anymore. You could have a Merzbow track next to an Ariana Grande track in the same playlist and feel no contradiction. Critics at the time called this the decade genre "collapsed," not because nothing new was made, but because the listening culture that used to sort people into scenes, the culture that turns a sound into an identity worth defending, had quietly stopped mattering. Lil Nas X could fuse rap and country and the "controversy" was a Billboard chart dispute, not a subcultural civil war.

SoundCloud rap arrived right at the tail end of that shift, and it's arguably the last genre to have a real scene behind it anyway: actual message boards, actual local shows, actual rivalries between artists who knew each other. By the time it burned out in 2019, it wasn't just that a specific sound had run its course. The underlying social structure that used to manufacture the next one, tribal, local, argument-heavy fan culture, had already been dissolving for most of a decade. SoundCloud rap wasn't just the last new genre. It was arguably the last time the old genre-making machine still had enough parts left to finish a job.

What COVID Actually Killed

COVID didn't stop people from making music. If anything, the opposite. With tours cancelled and studios closed, research on musicians during 2020 found a boom in solo, bedroom production. Instrument sales spiked, streaming numbers spiked, and self-releasing artists who didn't need a label's promotional calendar had, by some accounts, one of their best years ever for sheer output.

What COVID killed was the collaborative, in-person half of the equation: the part that turns individual output into a shared, argued-over scene. Studies of working musicians during lockdown found a real and measurable dip in collaboration. Fewer new co-writes, fewer people seeking out new collaborators, an initial period a lot of musicians described flatly as creative stagnation, before things slowly rebounded. Clubs closed. Pirate radio stations went quiet. Block parties didn't happen. The exact rooms where a new, half-formed, still-bad-sounding idea gets tested against a live crowd and refined into something with a name were shut for the better part of two years, globally, all at once, for the first time in the format's history.

So you got a strange split screen. More raw musical output than ever, funneled through fewer and fewer of the collective, local, repeated-testing environments that used to convert output into genre. The content kept flowing. The institution that used to sort it into something new mostly didn't survive the lockdown.

Going Viral Is Not the Same as Being Born

Here's the part that gets missed most often in "nothing new has happened since 2019" takes. It's not that nothing has happened. Amapiano is everywhere. Jersey club is inescapable. Drift phonk soundtracks half of TikTok. It's tempting to point at any of these and say, see, new genres are still happening. But look at where each of them actually came from.

None of these are new genres. They're old, real, regionally rooted genres that an algorithm finally decided to distribute globally, years or decades after they were actually invented. What changed in the 2020s wasn't the rate of invention. It's that the platforms got extremely good at excavating pre-existing underground sounds and shipping them to a global audience with a dance challenge attached. That's a genuinely different activity from what happened in Chicago basements in 1985 or Newark basements in 2003. It's distribution, not birth. We've mistaken the algorithm getting better at archaeology for culture getting better at invention.

Hyperpop and the Best Counterargument

Reality Check

If there's one honest exception to all this, it's hyperpop, and even hyperpop mostly proves the point once you look at how it actually formed. Its sonic roots trace to the UK's PC Music label in the mid-2010s, through A.G. Cook and SOPHIE. The duo most credited with defining it, 100 gecs, released their breakout album in May 2019, technically inside the SoundCloud rap era, not after it. The thing that actually turned it into a genre with a name wasn't a scene or a movement. It was a Spotify staff editor curating a playlist called "Hyperpop" in August 2019, which then picked up steam through 2020 largely because of "alt TikTok."

Sit with that for a second. The most cited "new genre of the 2020s" is, by its own origin story, a corporate playlist title retroactively applied to a scattered handful of artists who didn't necessarily think of themselves as one movement. That's not nothing (hyperpop is a real and interesting sound) but it's a different kind of thing than house or grime or SoundCloud rap. Genre used to name something a scene built. Now, at best, a platform names something first and a scene sometimes grows into the label afterward. That's the shift in one sentence: from genre to vibe. The vibe gets a name before it has an argument attached to it, and often never grows one.

The Algorithm Doesn't Want a New Genre

There's a structural reason platforms are better at exhuming old regional sounds than midwifing new ones, and it isn't a mystery. Recommendation systems are built to maximize engagement, and engagement rewards instant recognizability, not friction. A sound that's "similar to" something a listener already likes will always outperform a sound that requires them to sit with something genuinely strange for the first time, the same way punk or jungle sounded strange the first hundred times anyone heard it. TikTok's fifteen-second-hook economy has pushed songwriting toward front-loaded, instantly legible payoffs. Researchers analyzing decades of hit songs have found a real, measurable decline in melodic complexity since the 1950s, and a long-term drift toward what's called timbral homogenization: the overall sonic palette of popular music narrowing over time. Spotify's own "similar artists" logic works the same way in reverse. It's fantastic at deepening a groove you're already in, and structurally uninterested in the ugly, unresolved, not-yet-a-genre sound that a scene hasn't finished arguing about yet.

None of this requires a conspiracy. It's just what optimizing for engagement does when you point it at culture for long enough. It gets extremely efficient at giving people more of exactly what already works, and it has no mechanism at all for the slow, collective, often bad-sounding process that used to produce something new. The algorithm isn't hostile to new genres. It's just indifferent to them, which turns out to be just as effective at starving them.

The Same Flattening, Different App

Once you see that pattern (legible categories with real rituals attached, replaced by an undifferentiated, algorithmically optimized default) it's hard not to notice it running through dating too.

Courtship used to have its own version of genre: recognizable stages with names and rules attached. Dating, going steady, engaged, each one a shared vocabulary two people and their social circle could reference. Swipe-based apps replaced that with a single mechanism, deliberately built to resemble a slot machine, optimized the same way a For You page is optimized: for the next swipe, not for a resolved outcome. Tinder logged its single highest swipe day ever in March 2020, the same month the world went into lockdown, the dating equivalent of everyone being pushed onto the feed at once. What's emerged since isn't a new "genre" of relationship with its own name and rules. It's a handful of deliberately vague, low-commitment labels, the talking stage, the situationship, that describe not a new category so much as the absence of one: an undifferentiated default state that resists ever hardening into something with real rules, the same way a TikTok sound resists ever hardening into a genre with real rules.

Worth noting, because it complicates the story in a useful way: there's early evidence of pushback on both fronts. Surveys describe a small but real exodus from swipe-based apps, particularly among younger users who say they're tired of gamified, low-effort matching and want something with actual structure again. That's the same shape as small physical music scenes rebuilding in the post-lockdown years: a minority, deliberately opting out of the feed, back into rooms where things have to be worked out in person before they get a name.

Outrage Is the Last Reliable Genre We Have Left

The clearest place to watch this same mechanism operate at full strength is political and cultural discourse online, because there the "engagement over novelty" logic isn't even disguised. It's the whole business model. Platforms have openly built systems that boost whatever keeps people clicking, and the content that reliably does that isn't nuance or a genuinely new idea. It's anger, fear, and moral offense, because those are the emotional signals that cut through the noise fastest. Researchers studying this have given it the obvious name: the outrage economy. Content that names a clear enemy and demands a strong, immediate emotional reaction outperforms content that asks you to sit with something complicated, for the exact same reason a recognizable four-on-the-floor beat outperforms an unresolved, still-forming new sound. Outrage is, in effect, the one "genre" the current algorithmic environment still reliably manufactures, because unlike a new kind of music, it doesn't require years of a scene arguing itself into a shared vocabulary. It works instantly, on anyone, the first time.

Music, dating, and online discourse are three completely different domains that all ran into the same wall at the same moment, because it's the same wall. Systems built to optimize for immediate engagement have no mechanism for, and a mild structural hostility toward, anything that needs time, friction, and a real scene to become legible. New genres, real courtship, and reasoned argument all require exactly that kind of slow, collective, sometimes bad-sounding incubation. The feed doesn't have the patience for any of them.

Is It Actually Dead?

I don't think it's dead so much as it's been evicted from the places that used to house it, and the most honest closing note is that this may be temporary, not permanent, because the underlying human appetite for something genuinely new hasn't gone anywhere.

It's also worth being clear-eyed about where this trajectory leads next, because it's already here: AI music generation. Tools like Suno and Udio, now producing full, structured songs from a text prompt, are, by design, the purest expression of the remix logic this whole piece has been describing. Suno's own product now ships with well over a thousand genre tags built into its interface. The tools are explicitly built to interpolate within the space of sounds that already exist and have already been labeled, because that's what the underlying models were trained to do. That's not a criticism of the tools so much as a description of their ceiling. Recombination at genuinely impressive scale is still not the same mechanism as a scene arguing a new sound into existence over months of live, in-person friction. If anything, AI generation formalizes the shift this piece has been tracing: from genre as something a room full of people builds together, to genre as a tag you select from a dropdown before generation starts.

If a genuinely new genre does show up again, my guess is it won't come from a platform's recommendation engine or a chart-topping playlist. It'll come from the same place it always has: a room somewhere the algorithm hasn't found yet, where a sound gets to be bad, in public, in front of the same people, often enough times that it stops being bad and starts being new.
